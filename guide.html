How to Join

Your Guide to Troop 54

Click any topic below to expand it

Troop 54 uses the Band app for all troop communication. This is where you'll find meeting updates, event details, schedule changes, photos, and important announcements.

How to join: Download Band (free on iOS and Android), then join Troop 54 directly or search for "Troop 54" in the app. A leader will approve your request.

What you'll find on Band: Weekly meeting agendas, campout details and packing lists, permission slips, photos and recaps, direct messaging with leaders, and the calendar of upcoming activities.

Important: Band is our primary communication channel. If you're not on Band, you may miss time-sensitive updates. At least one parent and (if possible) your Scout should be members.

The safety of every Scout is our highest priority. Scouting America has clear policies that all adults and youth must follow.

Two-Deep Leadership: At every Scout activity - meetings, campouts, service projects, transportation - at least two registered adult leaders must be present. No adult should ever be alone with a youth who is not their own child.

Safeguarding Youth Training (SYT): As of May 2025, SYT replaced the older Youth Protection Training. All registered adult leaders must complete SYT before participating in any Scout activity. It's free at my.scouting.org, takes ~90 minutes, and can be paused and resumed. SYT is now required annually.

Family Troop requirement: At least one registered female adult leader (21+) must be present whenever female Scouts participate.

Troop 54 uses two types of uniform:

Class A (Field Uniform): Worn for formal events, Courts of Honor, boards of review, and when representing the troop in public. Includes the tan uniform shirt with patches, olive pants/shorts, Scout belt, and Troop 54 neckerchief.

Class B (Activity Uniform): Worn for regular meetings, service projects, and outdoor activities. A Troop 54 t-shirt with comfortable pants or shorts.

Where to buy: Twin Rivers Scout Shop, 253 Washington Ave Ext, Albany, NY 12205 (Mon-Fri 9:30 AM-5:30 PM, Sat 10 AM-2 PM). Also available at scoutshop.org. Used uniforms are often available through the troop at no cost - just ask a leader.

Don't stress about a perfect uniform right away. Start with the shirt and neckerchief, and add items as you go.

The patrol method is how Troop 54 operates. Scouts are organized into small groups called patrols, each with a youth Patrol Leader. As a Family Troop, our patrols are single-gender, but the entire troop participates in activities together.

How it works: Patrols plan and cook their own meals on campouts. Patrol Leaders attend a Patrol Leaders' Council (PLC) to plan troop activities. The Senior Patrol Leader (SPL) runs troop meetings with guidance from the Scoutmaster. Scouts elect their leaders and rotate through positions.

Youth leadership positions: Senior Patrol Leader (SPL), Assistant SPL, Patrol Leader, Troop Guide, Quartermaster, Scribe, and Instructor. Leadership time is required for Star, Life, and Eagle - so start building experience early.

For parents: It can be tempting to step in when things seem disorganized. The patrol method is intentionally youth-led. Mistakes are learning opportunities.

Scouting uses a rank system to help Scouts grow in skills, leadership, and character. Each rank builds on the last. Scouts advance at their own pace.

The ranks: Scout → Tenderfoot → Second Class → First Class → Star → Life → Eagle Scout. Getting to First Class within the first year is the first big milestone - it covers the core skills of knots, first aid, cooking, navigation, camping, and fitness.

How advancement works: Learn the skill → demonstrate it → get signed off in your handbook → Scoutmaster Conference → Board of Review (a friendly conversation, not a test). The rank is then awarded at the next Court of Honor.

Eagle Scout is the highest rank in Scouting - achieved by fewer than 6% of all Scouts. Visit the About page to see the full path to Eagle.

For Scouts: Advancement is YOUR responsibility. Track your progress, bring your handbook to every meeting, and ask for help when you need it.

Merit badges let Scouts explore over 130 topics - from Camping and First Aid to Robotics and Photography. Some are required for Eagle, others are electives.

The process:

  1. Pick a badge that interests you
  2. Talk to your Scoutmaster - they'll connect you with a Merit Badge Counselor
  3. Get a blue card signed by the Scoutmaster
  4. Contact the counselor (the Scout does this, not the parent)
  5. Complete the requirements with the counselor's guidance
  6. Counselor signs the blue card when all requirements are done
  7. Return the blue card to the troop - badge is awarded at Court of Honor

14 Eagle-required badges: First Aid, Citizenship in the Community/Nation/World/Society, Communication, Cooking, Personal Fitness, Emergency Preparedness or Lifesaving, Environmental Science or Sustainability, Personal Management, Swimming or Hiking or Cycling, Camping, and Family Life.

For Scouts: The blue card is YOUR responsibility. Don't lose it - losing one means starting over with paperwork.

Camping is at the heart of Scouting. Troop 54 aims to camp about once a month throughout the year.

What to expect: Patrols plan and cook their own meals, Scouts set up their own tents, and the SPL and Patrol Leaders run the campout. Activities include hiking, orienteering, skill workshops, and plenty of fun.

Summer camp: A week-long experience at a Scout camp each July. Scouts earn merit badges, develop skills, and have an incredible time.

Before every campout: Check Band for details and packing lists, make sure your BSA health form is current (Parts A, B, and C for overnight events), pack your own gear, and confirm transportation.

Family Troop camping: Separate tenting areas for male and female Scouts, separate restrooms/showers, and youth sharing a tent must be within two years of age. A female adult leader must be present when female Scouts are camping.

For parents: Let your Scout pack their own bag. They'll forget things - and that's how they learn. Next time, they'll remember.

The Order of the Arrow (OA) is Scouting's national honor society, recognizing Scouts who best exemplify the Scout Oath and Law. Being elected is a meaningful honor - chosen by fellow Scouts, not by adults.

Eligibility: First Class rank or higher, plus at least 15 nights of camping including one long-term camp of 5+ consecutive nights.

How it works: Annual election by secret ballot - at least 50% of active Scouts must be present. Elected Scouts attend an Ordeal weekend focused on service and reflection.

Troop 54 is part of Kittan Lodge, Naxa Nimat Chapter. The best preparation? Show up consistently, lead by example, and live the Scout Oath and Law.

Typical costs:

  • Annual registration and troop dues: ~$150/year
  • Campout fees: Vary by event (typically cover food and campsite)
  • Summer camp: Week-long fees (deposits may be required)
  • Uniform and gear: Purchased by each family

The troop conducts fundraisers throughout the year to offset costs - our annual popcorn sale is the biggest. All fundraisers are announced on Band.

Financial assistance: No Scout should miss out because of cost. If your family needs help with registration, camp fees, or gear, please speak with a troop leader privately. We will work with you.

As of 2026, Troop 54 is a Family Troop - welcoming both male and female Scouts under one charter, one committee, and one Scoutmaster. Everyone participates in the same activities, earns the same ranks, and has the same leadership opportunities.

Patrols: Organized by gender. Boys and girls are in separate patrols but participate in all troop activities together. The Senior Patrol Leader is elected from the full troop.

Adult leadership: All standard two-deep leadership rules apply. At least one registered female adult leader (21+) must be present whenever female Scouts participate. All registered adult leaders must have current SYT.

Camping arrangements: Separate tenting areas, separate restrooms/showers, and youth sharing a tent must be within two years of age. A parent may share a tent with their own child if same sex.

Buddy system: Same-gender buddy pairs of two are fine. A single boy and girl cannot be a buddy pair - a third Scout must be added for any mixed-gender group.

The Family Troop model is built on the same youth protection policies that have always been part of Scouting. Questions? Reach out to the Committee Chair or Scoutmaster.

What to Bring to Your First Meeting

Scout Handbook

If you have one - don't worry if you don't yet.

A Pen or Pencil

For taking notes and getting sign-offs.

A Positive Attitude

That's really it. Uniform shirt if you have one, but no pressure.

On your first night, you'll be assigned to a patrol - a small team of Scouts who work together. Your Patrol Leader will show you the ropes. Just relax and be yourself.
